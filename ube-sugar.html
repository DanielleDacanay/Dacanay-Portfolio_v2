The Product

Ube & Sugar Bakery’s website allows customers to customize and place dessert orders online for delivery or in-store pick-up. Ube & Sugar Bakery currently only takes orders through phone or from messages on social media. This is a problem for new customers who aren’t familiar with their products and offerings.

Project Overview

Challenge: Design a responsive website for a bakery that streamlines the browsing, ordering and checkout process for customers.

Role(s): UX Researcher, UI Designer

Responsibilities: Conduct interviews and usability studies, create wireframes and prototypes, test for accessibility and iterate on designs

Duration: January 2022 - February 2022 (5 weeks)

Tools: Figma, Adobe XD

User Research

In order to better understand the users and their needs, I conducted interviews and created empathy maps. A primary user group identified during the research was on-the-go full-time employees who have limited time to run errands. This user group confirmed some initial assumptions about dessert-lovers who browse menus online, but further research revealed that lack of customization and inflexible pick-up times were common challenges customers face in addition to counterintuitive navigation.

Pain Points

  • Time — Customers struggle to find a convenient pick-up/delivery time when placing last-minute custom cake orders
  • Navigation — Customers find it frustrating to browse menus online and struggle to place orders on platforms other than desktops
  • Customization — Customers who want custom cakes usually don’t see enough customization options online and must order in-store reservations quickly

Paper Wireframes

When iterating on the home screen, I prioritized ease of access and prominence of product photos. When choosing the elements to include in the refined version, I opted for elements that emphasized the bakery’s products and gave quick access to the main product offerings.

After refining the wireframe for the home screen, I created different iterations for various devices (desktop, tablet and mobile). When creating the different versions, I made sure that the key elements used for the desktop version were not lost or skewed. Each version prioritizes ease of use and visibility.

Digital Wireframes

Easy access to key actions was necessary in the home screen so that users can quickly find what they are looking for.

Usability Findings

Two rounds of usability studies were conducted – one moderated and one unmoderated. Findings from the first study helped uncover major pain points with the initial design. The second study used a high-fidelity prototype and revealed what elements still needed refinement.

Round 1 Findings

  • Users wanted feedback to confirm an item was added to their cart
  • Users were confused by the progress tracker in the checkout flow
  • Users wanted a way to order more than one of the same item

    • Round 2 Findings

  • Users found the confirmation message obstructive and wanted a way to keep browsing without acknowledging the message every time
  • Users wanted a way to see the order status

    Feedback

    Usability studies revealed that customers wanted actionable feedback when they added an item to their cart. The informational text was sufficient for some customers, but the majority of users wanted a way to actually verify the action and view their cart.

    Visual Cues

    The usability study showed that customers found the checkout progress tracker confusing and didn’t find it helpful at all when trying to figure out how many steps were left. Iconography and colors were used to better inform the customer of where they currently were and what was coming next.

    Accessibility Considerations

    In order to better understand the users and their needs, I conducted interviews and created empathy maps. A primary user group identified during the research was busy restaurant hosts who had more than one role on the staff. This user group confirmed some initial assumptions about restaurant hosts, but further research revealed that time constraints were only part of the causes for finding difficulty with bookkeeping. Other challenges included inconsistency and legibility.

    Language Support

    Added a language option so that customers could change the language used throughout the website.

    Contrast

    Changed the color palette and included higher contrasting colors to help users with reading difficulties or visual impairments.

    Readability

    Revised copy and button labels to be more descriptive and comprehensible for non-native speakers

    Takeaways & Next Steps

    Impact

    The website alleviates navigational frustrations and time constraints faced by customers who want to place an online order so that they can quickly locate and receive their desired dishes.

    Lessons Learned

    As I was designing this responsive website, I learned that my interpretation of what a good and useful design is does not necessarily mean that it is the best design for what users need. The end-users’ perceptions are ultimately the most reliable deciding factors for app design.
